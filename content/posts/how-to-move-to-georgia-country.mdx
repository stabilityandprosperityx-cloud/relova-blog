## Visa-free policy: what a one-year stamp actually lets you do

Citizens of the EU, U.K., U.S., Canada, Australia, and many others may receive **365-day visa-free stays** for tourism and general presence, subject to border officer discretion and proof of onward plans when asked. Visa-free status is not automatically a work authorization: remote work for foreign employers exists in a gray conversation globally, but local employment requires appropriate permits. If you intend to incorporate, hire locally, or stay beyond a year with cleaner status, pivot to a **residence permit** (work, investment, study, family reunification, or special categories like the digital nomad-style options Georgia has promoted during reform cycles—verify current law).

### When to apply for temporary residence anyway

Apply if you need multi-year stability for schools, long apartment contracts, or repeated Schengen-adjacent travel patterns where a stamped permit card helps banking KYC. Also apply if you plan to become tax resident and want paperwork aligned with your story.

## Banking: patience, paper trails, and power-of-attorney edge cases

Georgian banks tightened compliance after past years of high-volume foreign openings. Expect questions on employment, clients, and crypto history if relevant. A **printed** apartment contract, utility bill, and a letter explaining remote income shorten interviews. If you are U.S.-person flagged for FATCA, some institutions say no quickly—line up two bank options.

### Multi-currency habits

Many expats hold both GEL and USD accounts, using local cards day-to-day and Wise or similar for cross-border slices—confirm fee schedules yourself. Carry a backup card from your home country during onboarding hiccups.

## Healthcare: mix public, private, and evacuation thinking

Tbilisi private clinics like **Aversi, Vivamed, or Evex-branded facilities** (verify current reputations with recent arrivals) handle GP visits, labs, and many specialties in English. For complex conditions, people sometimes travel to Istanbul or Western Europe—budget emergency evacuation insurance if that matches your risk tolerance. Dental tourism is a real win here; orthodontics or implants often cost a fraction of Western quotes.

## Neighborhoods in Tbilisi: match your personality

### Vera, Vake, and Saburtalo

**Vake** offers leafy streets, dog-walking culture, and uphill cardio; **Vera** mixes restaurants and older charm with some noise; **Saburtalo** delivers Soviet blocks and newer towers, good metro access, and expat density. If you want quiet, confirm nightclub proximity—Tbilisi bass travels.

### Old Tbilisi

Picturesque and tourist-heavy; charming for month one, noisy for year two unless you pick side streets carefully and verify building insulation.

### Dighomi and suburbs

More parking, newer builds, family-friendly vibes, but you live in taxis unless you enjoy driving chaotic traffic.

## How to move to Georgia country without ruining your first month

Treat month one as logistics, not tourism only. Open a local SIM immediately (Magti or Cellfie—compare current data promos), register your address if your permit path requires it, and locate the nearest pharmacy chain that stocks your prescriptions or acceptable substitutes. Carry small lari notes for bakeries and marshrutkas; cards work in many venues but not all courtyards. If you ship household goods, budget customs patience and hire a broker for anything beyond a few boxes.

## Weather, shoes, and the romance trap

Tbilisi summers hit thirties Celsius with dry heat; winters are damp-cold, not glamorous snow unless you drive to Gudauri. Pack shoes with grip for polished tiles and uneven sidewalks. The cheap wine is excellent; pace yourself—integration goes better with clear mornings.

## How to move to Georgia country with pets

Georgia allows many cats and dogs with vaccination paperwork and sometimes titer tests depending on origin country rules. Airlines have summer embargoes for snub-nosed breeds. Line up a vet who speaks English, and confirm landlord pet policies before you pay deposits—many new towers are strict.

## Working remotely: practical realities

Power cuts happen; buildings with **generators** deserve a line item in rent. Buy a small UPS for your router and modem. Fiber installs sometimes take two visits—schedule work calls with buffers. Coworking spaces like Terminal, Space Z, and smaller boutique hubs provide backup power and community; drop-in day passes help before you commit monthly.

## Food, khinkali etiquette, and the expat tax

Eat widely: **khinkali** dumplings are judged by juice retention, **khachapuri** by cheese pull, and home-style **mtsvadi** by smoke patience. That said, if you default only to expat-priced brunch spots in Vake, your cost table balloons. Learn three Georgian words for “less salt” or “no cilantro” if needed. Carry cash for small bakeries; tip modestly where service is personal, not automatic.

## Driving, licenses, and why many people skip a car in Tbilisi

International Driving Permits paired with a valid home license often work for tourists short-term; long-stayers should confirm current reciprocity and local conversion rules. Parking in central districts is tight; sidewalks double as lanes during rush hour. Many remote workers rely on Bolt and metro plus occasional rentals for mountain trips—renting a 4x4 for Kazbegi weekends beats owning depreciating metal you barely use.

## Taxes for long-stayers: do not DIY from Reddit

Georgia’s tax system offers low flat personal rates for certain classifications and special schemes for individual entrepreneurs with small-business status, but eligibility is statutory, not vibes. If you stay **183+ days** or trigger other tests, you may become tax resident. Engage a **local Georgian accountant** before you invoice clients through a local IE structure.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long can Americans stay in Georgia visa-free?**  
Many passport holders receive up to one year visa-free for qualifying purposes, but border officers can shorten stays if they doubt intent; carry proof of funds, insurance, and onward tickets when asked.

**Q: Is Georgia safe for solo relocators?**  
Most expats report an easy day-to-day experience with normal urban precautions; petty theft occurs in crowded tourist pockets. Learn basic local etiquette and avoid drunk driving entirely—enforcement and mountain roads are unforgiving.

**Q: Can I open a bank account on a tourist stamp?**  
Sometimes yes with strong KYC packages; other times banks want residence cards. Plan two bank visits and avoid Friday afternoons before holidays.

**Q: How much money should I budget for the first two months?**  
A single person might allocate $4,000–$7,000 including deposit, rent, insurance, and setup friction; couples scale higher. Carry a cushion for currency swings.

**Q: Is moving to Georgia (the country) realistic for families with kids?**  
Yes—international schools exist in Tbilisi with tuition bands from mid thousands to teen thousands of dollars annually; visit schools before paying deposits because waitlists and philosophies differ widely.
