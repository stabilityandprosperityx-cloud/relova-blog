## Visa overview: do not work on the wrong stamp

### Tourist entry

Many passports receive **90-day** entries renewable in policy patterns that change—verify current immigration circulars. Tourism is for tourism; performing paid work for Colombian clients without permission risks fines and bans.

### Digital nomad visa (M visa category conversation)

Colombia has marketed routes for remote workers paid abroad. You will present income thresholds, insurance, criminal records, and application fees. Processing timelines often land in **two to six weeks** with clean files, longer if translations lag. Confirm whether your consulate wants appointments in advance.

### Resident paths: work, marriage, investment, parentage

Traditional work offers yield employer-backed visas. Investment or business routes require capital and legal structuring. Marriage and family ties follow documentary evidence. Pick the story that matches your bank statements.

## Neighborhoods: match hills and patience

**El Poblado** (Provenza, Manila) delivers restaurants, English, and nightlife at premium rent and slope-heavy walks.**Laureles** offers flatter streets, local cafes, and strong value—popular with long-haul nomads.**Envigado** suits families wanting space and quieter nights.**Centro** is cheaper and grittier—visit at multiple hours before committing.**Belén** and southern zones vary block by block—use a local realtor with ethics references, not only Instagram.

## Safety: situational awareness without catastrophizing

Petty phone theft and occasional scams appear in nightlife districts. Use in-app taxis, avoid drunk wandering with visible jewelry, and learn Spanish phrases for police help. Rural weekend trips have different road and weather risks—check motorcycle gear if you ride. **Moving to Colombia Medellín expat** forums skew alarmist; talk to neighbors who pay rent year-round.

## Healthcare: private hospitals and insurance

Clinica Santa Fe, Pablo Tobón Uribe, and similar private networks offer strong outpatient care; major surgeries still prompt some expats to travel regionally. Buy insurance that pays hospitals directly if possible. Bring prescription paperwork; controlled substances follow import rules.

## Banking and money

Open accounts as law allows with your visa progress; some banks welcome residents more than fresh arrivals. Keep Wise or similar for cross-border slices; declare flows if your home country requires. Carry two cards—skimming still happens.

## Spanish: the unfair advantage

Life opens after **A2**: leases, repairs, dates, and cheaper groceries. Study **one hour daily** for ninety days before arrival; iTalki tutors cost less than one bad landlord dispute.

## Medellín vs Bogotá vs Cali: quick orientation

**Bogotá** offers more corporate HQs, colder nights, and heavier traffic—salaries can be higher but rent bites too.**Cali** brings salsa culture and lower costs with different security microclimates.**Medellín** balances climate, international community, and direct flights—if your clients are Americas-heavy, timezone wins matter more than European café aesthetics.

## Income documentation expats actually submit

For nomad-style paths, consulates often want **last three to six months** of foreign bank statements, employer or client letters, and tax returns if you are U.S.-based. Lumpy crypto income needs a narrative—expect questions. Translate documents with certified translators when mandated; DIY Google Docs fail.

## Comparison: cash vs card daily life

Small tiendas prefer cash; supermarkets and apps take cards. Carry small COP notes for tips and buses. Rappi and Uber mask complexity—learn basic dispute Spanish for wrong orders.

## Tax residency and DIAN basics

Long stays may trigger Colombian tax residency tests. If you buy property, rental income and eventual capital gains add local filing layers even when you still file abroad. Remote foreign salary may still have reporting angles—hire a Colombian accountant if you stay **183+ days** or buy property. Treat cross-border advice as mandatory, not optional, once you own assets or hire local assistants.

## Remote employer notifications

Tell HR you are relocating—payroll tax and permanent establishment questions follow you. Some employers approve Colombia; others restrict LATAM countries. Get written remote-work authorization before you sign a twelve-month lease.

## Deposits, fiadores, and lease negotiation

Landlords sometimes request guarantors (**fiadores**) or larger deposits for foreigners without local credit history. Offer additional months prepaid only when contracts are ironclad and receipts are official. Photograph unit condition meticulously; disputes over paint scuffs drain patience. If a listing looks too cheap, verify utility inclusion and building administration (**administración**) fees—they can dwarf rent savings.

## Culture notes that save embarrassment

Punctuality flexes socially; dress slightly sharper for immigration appointments. Tip modestly in cafes; read room-specific norms. Discussing narco tropes with strangers is tedious—locals deserve better conversation starters.

## When agents promise “instant” visas

If someone asks for cash in a parking lot, walk away. Use registered lawyers with clear invoices tied to government fee schedules you can verify.

## Schools and raising kids in the valley

International schools cluster around southern municipalities with tuition often **$6,000–$18,000+** yearly per child. Waitlists tighten in January–March for August starts. If you are **moving to Colombia Medellín expat** life with teens, verify AP/IB availability and bus routes before you sign a lease on a pretty mountaintop finca with ninety-minute commutes.

## Pets: apartments and terraces

Many buildings restrict large dogs or charge deposits. Ask about noise rules—barking complaints escalate quickly in dense towers. Vet care is affordable; import paperwork for international arrivals must be precise.

## Coffee culture as networking

Medellín’s café scene doubles as business development. Arrive early for quiet calls; afternoons fill with meetings. Carry a lightweight jacket—sun-warm valleys cool fast in rain.

## Gym costs and altitude

Gyms run **$25–$80** monthly depending on brand. Altitude can humble cardio the first month—hydrate and sleep more than you think you need.

## Domestic travel: Cartagena, coffee belt, Eje Cafetero

Cheap flights reward weekend exploration; pack layers—microclimates vary wildly. Keep digital copies of visa stamps when domestic airlines check ID patterns.

## Coworking and noise-canceling reality

Selina, WeWork-class spaces, and boutique coworks dot Poblado and Laureles. Book monthly memberships after a week of day passes—noise and chair quality differ more than Wi-Fi speeds.

## Apartment hunt red flags

Mold behind sofas, water pressure at peak hours, and “included utilities” that exclude gas spikes. Insist on written contracts even when landlords prefer informal trust; eviction disputes favor documentation.

## Dating and social life

Apps work; language level changes match quality. Join salsa classes even if you have two left feet—social graphs expand fastest through repeated weekly rooms, not one-off bar nights.

## Shipping household goods

Containers rarely beat buying locally unless you own specialized gear. Sell bulky furniture, bring laptops and heirlooms in carry-ons, and rebuy mattresses that understand Colombian humidity.

## Political and economic awareness

Election cycles and protest calendars affect road closures; follow local news in Spanish. Currency moves swing rent equivalences—negotiate annual leases with sane escalation clauses, not blind trust.

## Volunteer work and visa caution

Unpaid volunteering can still implicate labor rules depending on visa class—ask counsel before you commit weekly hours to NGOs.

## Photography and drone etiquette

Mountain vistas tempt drones; privacy and no-fly rules apply. Ask building admins before filming common pools—you are a guest, not invisible.

## Leaving Colombia cleanly

Close bank accounts with written confirmations, settle utility final bills, and scan exit stamps. Future visa applications may ask travel history—gaps invite questions.
